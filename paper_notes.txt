The microcontroller functions as the central control unit of the FES system, controlling the generation of stimulation waveforms and the management of channel-specific parameters. The firmware stores stimulation settings, including pulse width, frequency, leading amplitude, and following amplitude for each channel. Additionally, the microcontroller oversees the time-division demultiplexing of waveforms to efficiently distribute signals across multiple channels.

The time-division demultiplexing algorithm treats every pulse as a task that needs to be executed every period seconds for the duration of the pulse, akin to scheduling programs for a computer. Higher priority is given to pulses that are scheduled earlier. As some blocking of the channel can occur, the program prioritizes ensuring the average frequency of the signal is maintained to desired frequency, ie. if a channel's pulse is delayed, then the next pulse will be scheduled sooner.

Real-time adjustments to stimulation parameters are enabled through a serial communication interface, allowing dynamic control during operation. In this study, the microcontroller was programmed to generate two square pulses separated by an interphase interval (IPI) of 50 µs. The pulse width and frequency are determined by values received via serial communication, while the amplitude is regulated using a 5-bit R-2R ladder digital-to-analog converter (DAC).

To ensure precise timing, the system implements priority-based execution, where overlapping or concurrent pulses are resolved by executing the earliest scheduled pulse first. Furthermore, the system maintains long-term frequency stability, ensuring that the average stimulation rate remains consistent over extended periods. Given the typical FES parameters—such as pulse widths below 1 ms and frequencies up to 50 Hz—the microcontroller operates well within its capacity, avoiding saturation and ensuring reliable performance.

The FES system incorporates a graphical user interface (GUI, Fig. 7) designed to streamline the configuration of stimulation parameters. Users can adjust the pulse width within a range of 0–500 µs, set the frequency between 0 and 100 Hz, and define the amplitude as an integer value from 0 (no current) to 15 (maximum current). Charge balance is preserved by ensuring that the product of pulse width and amplitude remains equal for both phases of the biphasic waveform.

Communication between the GUI and the FES device occurs via a serial interface, with instructions transmitted as space-separated UTF-8 values formatted as <channel> <pulse_width> <frequency> <amplitude> <falling_amplitude>, followed by a newline character. The system operates at a baud rate of 115200 to ensure efficient data transmission.

For advanced users and developers, the system offers an application programming interface (API) for direct control, enabling manual programming of stimulation parameters through serial commands. Future enhancements may introduce additional customization features and deeper integration with therapeutic protocols, further expanding the system’s versatility.
